Brand guidelines

01 · Color

Palette

Four warm neutrals and three accent hues. Each accent comes in a light fill and a deep tone. Cream carries the page, Ink does the talking. Magenta is the warm signature; teal is the secondary system accent; soft coral is gentle warm support. Use the role tokens (--bg, --text, --accent, --link) in real pages, not the raw hex.

Update · the discipline rule. Accents are magenta and teal only. Magenta (pink and deep pink) carries emphasis, numbers, X marks, and key-phrase highlights. Teal (mint and deep mint) owns the structural and connective work: panel left-borders, timeline lines and nodes, checkmarks. Soft coral stays a quiet warm support for backgrounds and blocks, never for meaning. No green, the green checkmarks were a drift and are now teal. No stray colors.

Working rules

Two or three text colors

Ink for primary, Warm Gray for muted, Deep Pink for links and emphasis. Deep Mint and Deep Coral are optional accent-text extras.

60 / 30 / 10

Roughly 60% cream, 30% Ink, 10% accent. The accents are seasoning. When in doubt, use less of them.

Light and dark tiers

Contrast only exists between tiers. Light text on a light background never works. Put text on Ink, or Ink on light, or use a deep tone.

Tone-on-tone for depth

Pink card with Deep Pink text, Mint with Teal, Coral with Deep Coral. Same hue, far apart in lightness. The considered, layered look.

Magenta and teal, two jobs

Magenta is the signature for emphasis and highlights. Teal is the secondary accent for structure: panels, timelines, checks. Coral is a quiet warm support. Magenta and teal only carry meaning.

Meaning in Ink, mood in accents

Anything that must be read at a glance on a light page lives in Ink or a deep tone, never in a pastel. Pastels are decoration.

Do

  • Pair a Deep Pink, Teal, or Deep Coral heading with Ink body on cream.
  • Use an Ink section with a Pink headline for high energy.
  • Give pastel cards a hairline border so they read on cream.
  • Make primary buttons Ink, secondary buttons a Pink fill.
  • Use Warm Gray for captions and metadata, not faded Ink.

Don't

  • Set Pink, Mint, or Coral as text on cream.
  • Introduce green, or any color outside magenta and teal accents.
  • Rely on the Cream / Cream Soft difference to separate sections.
  • Use more than three text colors in one layout.
  • Let key information live in a pastel accent color.

02 · Typography

Type

Three typefaces, each with one job. Golden Batch sets the headlines. Hanken Grotesk handles everything you read in paragraphs, buttons, and labels. Caveat is a casual marker script used only for small handwritten accent words, never body text. All three are self-hosted, so they load fast and look the same everywhere.

The one rule for Golden Batch: regular (400) only. Never bold it. Bold crowds the letters and loses the elegance. The token sets font-synthesis: none so the browser cannot fake a bold weight either. When you need emphasis in a heading, use size or color, not weight.

Caution · swashes collide at large sizes. Golden Batch has swash glyphs that overlap neighboring letters and any adjacent emoji once a heading gets big (the swash on "This", the "r" in "right", an emoji butting the title). For large display headings, add a little letter-spacing (about 0.012em) and give emoji their own space with a flex gap rather than inlining them in the text. Check the rendered headers, not just the markup.

05 · Page patterns

How pages are built

The standards the homepage set: one text column, ombre on alternate sections, overlap at a few moments, and photos paired with color blocks.

Break the center axis

Not everything is centered. Centered is fine for short horizontal strips and the occasional section header. Body sections break left or sit asymmetric, off-center, so the page does not read as a stack of centered blocks. A centered hero followed by a left-broken section below it is the contrast we want. The reading column is still about 680px, it just does not have to sit dead-center.

Overlap and layering

The primary "designed by a person, not a template" lever. Photos break their frames, oversized numerals overlap panels, and accents cross boundaries instead of sitting politely inside them. Used at a few moments, not everywhere.

Panels, not white cards

Containment is done with panels: a teal left-accent, a very subtle tint, minimal border-radius, left-aligned. The banned pattern is the white rounded card on cream, repeated and centered, which is the main reason a page reads templated. The two, side by side:

Ombre on alternate sections

Cream is the base. Alternate sections carry a soft top-to-bottom ombre (cream into a light accent); the sections between stay solid cream or cream-soft. The Mint testimonial and Ink CTA are solid color bands.

Overlapping sections

A few intentional moments lift over the previous section with a rounded top (about 28 to 40px) and a soft upward shadow: the content rising over the hero, and the Mint and Ink bands. Not every section.

Photos and color blocks

Portraits sit on a soft panel with an offset accent block behind and a marker-script note on top. Hero and bio use background-removed cut-outs so nothing crops awkwardly.

hi!

Motion and engagement

A very subtle paper grain sits over the page (about 4% opacity) for warmth. Motion is quiet and earns its place. Restraint is the credibility signal, the brand is anti-hype.

Use

  • Scroll reveals: a fade plus a small rise on section entry, one easing, fast.
  • Count-up on key stats when scrolled into view, firing once.
  • A sticky clarity-call CTA that stays reachable.
  • Content readable without JS, and motion that honors prefers-reduced-motion.

Avoid

  • Scroll-jacking or hijacking the scroll position.
  • Heavy parallax and cursor-follow effects.
  • Autoplay-heavy motion that competes for attention.
  • Any animation that gates content until it finishes.

07 · Imagery

What we show

Favor real, credible imagery: ad dashboards, lead-form mockups, behind-the-scenes shots of the actual work. It doubles as proof. Avoid influencer-lifestyle photo density and stock that could sit on any page.

08 · Voice

How we write

Warm, direct, plain. Write the way you would talk to a client across the table. Speak to the reader as you, and be specific instead of vague.

Sounds like us

"You run the vision. I run the rest. One person who can hold the whole thing."

Not us

"We leverage a seamless, robust funnel to elevate and unlock your brand's full potential."

Do

  • Talk to the reader: you, your, yours.
  • Be specific. Real numbers, real timelines.
  • Keep sentences short and plain.
  • Sound warm and sure, not salesy.

Don't

  • No em dashes. Use a period or a comma.
  • No anaphora, no repeated sentence-opening rhythm.
  • No antithetical parallelism ("not this, but that").
  • No AI-sounding filler: elevate, unlock, leverage, seamless, robust.
  • No vague promises you cannot back up. Sentence case throughout.
